Solid surfaces explained: Corian, quartz, and where each wins

7 min read · The Walls Editorial · April 2025

The term "solid surface" gets used loosely in Dubai — often to describe anything that isn't natural stone. In practice, there are two distinct material families used in residential and commercial fit-outs: acrylic solid surfaces (like Corian, Hi-Macs, and Staron) and engineered quartz (like Silestone, Caesarstone, and Kompac). They look similar at first glance but perform very differently.

Here's what you need to know before specifying either material.

What is acrylic solid surface (Corian)?

Corian — DuPont's brand name that became generic — is a blend of acrylic polymer and alumina trihydrate. It's homogeneous throughout: cut it, and the inside looks the same as the surface. This matters because damage can be sanded and repaired invisibly.

Key properties:

  • Can be thermoformed into curves — ideal for reception desks, curved kitchen islands, and seamless runs
  • Non-porous and hygienic — meets food safety and medical standards
  • Scratches and minor damage are repairable with fine sanding and polishing
  • Available in matte finishes that don't show fingerprints
  • Not heat-resistant — hot pans damage the surface
  • Less hard than quartz — more prone to scratching in heavy-use kitchens

In Dubai, acrylic solid surfaces are widely used in office fit-outs for reception counters, breakout areas, and bathroom vanities. The ability to create seamless, curved forms with invisible joints is unmatched by any other material at its price point.

What is engineered quartz?

Engineered quartz (commonly called "quartz stone" in the UAE) is approximately 93% ground quartz bound with 7% polymer resins. It's harder than acrylic solid surface, more scratch-resistant, and can closely mimic the appearance of natural marble and granite.

Key properties:

  • Very hard surface — resists scratches from daily kitchen use
  • Heat-resistant to approximately 150°C (but sustained heat contact should still be avoided)
  • Non-porous — doesn't require sealing like natural stone
  • Available in large slab formats that minimise visible seams
  • Cannot be thermoformed — straight lines and flat surfaces only
  • Damage is not repairable — chips and cracks require full panel replacement

The head-to-head comparison

For kitchen countertops

Quartz wins. The hardness and heat resistance are meaningful in a kitchen environment. We specify quartz for all residential kitchen worktops unless the client specifically wants a matte, soft-touch surface, in which case acrylic becomes a close contender.

For bathroom vanities

Acrylic solid surface wins. Integrated sink bowls with no visible seam or silicone joint are possible only with thermoformed acrylic. The seamless joint eliminates mould growth in humid bathrooms — a major consideration in Dubai.

For commercial reception desks and curved forms

Acrylic solid surface wins decisively. No other material matches its formability at this price point. The ability to wrap a reception desk in a single continuous sheet — no joints, no seams — creates a level of finish that impresses clients and visitors.

For commercial kitchen and food preparation

Acrylic solid surface often wins here too, because its non-porous, repairable nature meets food safety requirements and allows long runs without joints where bacteria can accumulate.

Cost comparison in Dubai (2025)

Installed prices vary widely by supplier and specification, but as a general guide:

  • Entry-level engineered quartz (Silestone standard range): AED 380–550/sqm installed
  • Premium engineered quartz (Dekton, Neolith): AED 700–1,100/sqm installed
  • Entry-level acrylic solid surface (Corian standard): AED 350–500/sqm installed
  • Premium acrylic solid surface (Hi-Macs, Staron): AED 450–650/sqm installed

For most residential kitchens, mid-range quartz at AED 450–600/sqm installed is the right specification. For bathrooms and commercial applications, acrylic at AED 400–550/sqm will serve you better.

Our recommendation

Specify materials for the application, not the trend. Quartz in a kitchen, acrylic in a bathroom — it's that simple in most cases. Where it gets interesting is in commercial projects where form and seamlessness matter as much as durability: there, acrylic solid surface consistently delivers results that quartz cannot.
